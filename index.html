    Software Learning

    For the past few years, I have been trying to learn more about how the web and software works.

    Turns out its pretty interesting stuff, so I have been trying to make some fun projects and enter the software development field!

    The Odin Project

    After graduating from high school and dealing with the lockdown from COVID, I was trying to find new things to fill my freetime and decided I wanted to make a website.

    It didn't take long for me to feel a bit overwhelmed from a few google searches on the topic, so I looked for a resource that broke up the process into more managable parts for me. That resource ended up being The Odin Project.

    Following along with its extensive (at least in my opinion) curriculum, I was able to make a handful of small projects that helped me get a good grasp of the basic processes involved in web development.

    While I still want to finish the full stack curriculum offered on its website. I like to think the progress I've made so far has allowed to be a dangerous player in the webdev game.

    Dangerous to myself at least.

    Inkhollow

    With all the material I learned from The Odin Project and other resources, I got antsy and started making a website called Inkhollow.

    My plan for this website is pretty simple. I want to make a digital library that people can use to read if they have difficulty accessing books by more conventional means.

    I'm really excited about it because I plan on testing out a concept of voluntary ad exposure. Unlike a majority of other content-sharing platforms, my goal will be to avoid passive ad exposure and instead make it possible for readers to actively choose to view ads in order to support authors they enjoy.

    I have no clue how it will actually turn out, but I'm really curious if such an approach could offer a nicer experience for people that like to read online.
