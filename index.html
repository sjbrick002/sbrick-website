    Learning Software

    For the past few years, I have been learning more about how the web and software in general works.

    Turns out it's pretty interesting stuff, so I have been making some fun projects in order to enter the software development field!

    The Odin Project

    After graduating from high school in 2020, I was finding new things to fill my freetime and decided I wanted to make a website.

    It didn't take long for me to feel a bit overwhelmed from a few google searches on the topic, so I looked for a resource that broke up the process into more managable parts. That resource ended up being The Odin Project.

    Following along with its expanding curriculum, I was able to make a handful of challenging projects that helped me get a good grasp of the basic processes involved in web development.

    While I'm still finishing the full stack curriculum offered by the Odin Project, I think the progress I've made so far has allowed me to become a much more competent player in the webdev game.

    Inkhollow

    With all the material I learned from The Odin Project and other resources, I got an itch to make something completely separate from any of its assignments and started making an original website called Inkhollow.

    My plan for this website is pretty simple. I want to make a digital library that people can use to read if they have difficulty accessing books by more conventional means.

    I'm really excited about it because I plan on testing out a concept of voluntary ad exposure. Unlike a majority of other content-sharing platforms, my goal will be to avoid passive ad exposure and instead make it possible for readers to actively choose to view ads in order to support authors they enjoy.

    I have no clue how it will actually turn out, but I'm really curious if such an approach could offer a nicer experience for people that like to read online.

    Inspiration for this project comes from the itch.io and drivethrurpg platforms. Each site have pay-what-you-want features for their products, which gives users control over how they choose to support the service.
